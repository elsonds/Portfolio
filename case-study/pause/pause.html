Improving Day 1 Retention by trimming the onboarding flow

 

Apps see as much as 50% drop-off during onboarding

Acquiring new users is hard. 66% of consumers download zero apps in a month. And when they do try out products, 50% of them drop off during onboarding! The cost of acquiring a long-term user is high, and every attempt to improve the onboarding experience goes a long way towards product success.
An opportunity to tackle this challenge presented itself when I worked on a tool called Pause.

 

Pause is a time-off management and team planning tool, that helps teams: ⏳ take time off,💡 know colleagues' availability, 🎳 create transparent policies, 🤝 plan better together.

Challenge

How to reduce drop-off during onboarding?

The initial versions of the onboarding experience for Pause were meticulously crafted.
The flow involved steps to:

  • Create an Organization
  • Create leave policies
  • Invite members
  • Credit allowance
  • Organize teams
However, the process took several minutes to complete, and this was a cause of concern.

Approach

We took upon this challenge and looked for ways to improve the flow. We talked to our users and identified that our early adopters largely fit in two buckets: 1) Agencies 2) Early-stage startups with small teams. Narrowing the target groups helped us focus on the needs of these particular segments.

 

We identified certain parts of our onboarding flow that were better suited for large organizations. Trimming these down helped us shorten the flow.
When talking to users, we chanced upon another interesting insight that would help us immensely: most of our users either used G-suite or Slack. This was great, for two reasons:
1. We could use the organization metadata from Slack and G-Suite to pre-fill form fields in our flow.

 

Project Overview

designMYspace is a visual-aid mobile application that provides a low barrier of entry for novices to envision their dream home and communicate their ideas effectively and efficiently.

There were many challenges to tackle in this area. Hence, it was crucial to analyse market competitors. With a coupled of established visual-aid applications in the market, we would have to differentiate our app apart from these competitors.

While many of these applications are robust in their features, they present a steep learning curve. Users are needed to have specific knowledge and understanding to work on it.

To understand the users more, we conducted user interviews with targeted audiences before developing the concept for designMYspace.

In the research, we analysed their emotions and experiences as they recount their journey. We studied the pain points and discussed the possible resolutions to address them.

The goal of the user research was to find new potentials for innovation in the digital interior design area. The findings of the research shaped the ground for the concept of designMYspace.

 
 

Project Process

We followed this design process closely.

Problem Statement - The birth of the problem statement derived from my renovation experiences. I honestly struggled to pitch my idea across to my ID and the rectifications of the 3D design put me off. It was inefficient and time-consuming.

Observation - Given the tight deadlines, we managed to conduct user interviews with approximately 6 users within a week. Through these interviews we managed to deduce behaviours, draw insights & findings, before making a conclusive conclusion to it.

Prototyping - Crafted Low-Fi/High-Fi visuals and prototype it

Evaluation - Finally, we conducted A/B testings and evaluate them against different metrics to measure our success.

 
 

Problem Statement

How might we allow homeowners to envision their living space effectively and efficiently?

 
 

Observation Summary

It was a semi-structured interview. We conducted these interviews with a hands-on activity. We gave the interviewees an empty chart, which they were required to map out their emotions on the graph.

We got them to recount experiences which we identified the various pitfalls user had identified. This allowed us to probe even further, drawing more insights and behaviours.

In my opinion, this hands-on activity was an interesting way to start the conversation. Unlike the traditional interview that may come as an abrupt start, this hands-on activity served as a way for users to recall their renovation journey.

Persona

Informed by our research, we created a provisional persona. Annabelle Chan, to represent our main users, their needs, frustrations and goals. This information will be contextual to the hypothesized user and relevant to the value proposition.

 
 

Proposed Solution

A low-barrier of entry visual-aid application that provides homeowners to visualise their space, eliminating any unmet expectations.

 

Homeowners are able to take a photo of their floor plan layout. Based on the measurement, the application will automatically replicate the floor plan into a 2D floor grid without the need to have prior knowledge in rendering it.

designMYspace also provides curated list of templates to aid in homeowners’ design process. Very often, homeowners took to online platforms to gather different house themes and that requires a vast amount of effort to research. This feature would bridge the gap by providing homeowner a base of information to work on to further customise it.

 
 
 

There’s also a list of contractors which homeowners could turn to and get contractors to execute their house design should they do away with an interior designer. This solves the problem which homeowners need to source reliable contractors to execute their idea.

Technologies to Convert Photo to Digital Format

The first user-flow requires our user to capture a floor plan of their home.

We were immediately inspired by technologies that could convert a captured photo into a digital format. We decided to incorporate AdobeScan’s technologies - an application that eliminates the manual process of scanning documents.

We intend to incorporate a similar concept whereby the user can take a snapshot of their house floor plan from any angle.

We considered the feature where AdobeScan could automatically pick up the edges of a document so that capturing it need not be directly parallel from the camera sensor.

Since we require our home layout to be rendered in 3D, we decided to take a step beyond AdobeScan and explore technologies that drive photo-to-3D capabilities. We found Smoothie-3D and decided to integrate similar technology for our capture-to-render home layout feature.

Since modelling and creating a 3D-house layout requires a particular skill set in this area and could potentially require a large amount of effort in rendering such a model even for experts, this feature could directly eliminate such tedious tasks.

 
 
 
 

Roomle’s Floor Planner

After obtaining a blueprint of the homeowner’s house layout from the photo-taking, our application will render a 3D-visualisation for users to have an overall view of their house and how each of their rooms look like.

Visualisation of Home Layout
Initially, we wanted our application to render only a 3D-home-layout. Nevertheless, exploring both ‘Roomle’ application and its Floor Planner feature, in particular, it inspired us to provide our users with both 2D and 3D views of their home.

We felt that this could allow users carry out a two-pronged approach to make decisions where 2D view facilitates decision-making based on the overall spatial limit, while 3D facilitates decision-making based on spatial distribution and arrangement among house components.

We also thought that it is a good idea to follow Roomle’s provision of dimensions across every house edge.

This visualisation approach allows users to experiment in both 2D and 3D views to build, arrange, edit and furnish their rooms from various angles.

 
 
 
 

‘Presets’ from Photo Editors

In addition to providing ease of viewing users’ house layout and inspecting things like spatial restrictions, we would also like to provide ease of customisation.

House Design Templates
We were inspired by photo editing applications that provide a comprehensive set of “presets” or “filters” to quickly change the look of a photo with a single tap.

These preset combined a custom set of pre-configured adjustments of say, white balance, contrast, and etc. so that users do not need to go through the manual and tedious task of adjusting by themselves.

Similar to these photo editing applications, we decided to give users the flexibility to select from a list of curated home design templates and apply it to their 3D-modelled home layouts.

In addition, users also get to select from a list of commercialised interior-design themes to their 3D models.

Once applied, the theme will be loaded with furniture to complement the style. We deemed this feature important because templates help to streamline the entire choosing process, saving time.

 
 
 
 

Augmented Reality (AR) Technology

Augmented reality allows computer graphics to be drawn and overlaid onto a person’s physical view of the surrounding.

With such technology, we were inspired by how augmented reality has revolutionised the Interior Design industry where it could potentially take out the very human element - an Interior Designer - which leaves the majority of house planning to a homeowner.

Viewing Templates & Furnitures in Real-World Environment
Since our application largely deals with planning, and visualisation, we felt that it is imperative to incorporate augmented reality into our features: (1) viewing of house layout within the actual surrounding, (2), applying house templates and view through AR in real-time and (3) reviewing size and placements of furniture by panning and zooming.

Our design considerations are drawn from the likes of ‘IKEA Place’ App and Wayfair App. Through the ‘IKEA Place’ app, we were inspired by the ease of selecting a furnishing from a display of IKEA’s catalogue and adding it to where the camera is pointed to in the surrounding. This ties in with our user-flow of selecting a list of house templates and/or furniture and allowing them to place it anywhere on the screen.

Typography

Our application uses Libre Franklin as the primary typography. Libre Franklin is an open-source font, free on Google Fonts.

It is a versatile sans-serif, suitable for both long-form text and headlines. It has an extensive amount of font weights (ranging from Black to extra light) in the font family which is very handy for a designer to play around with the contrast and its hierarchy.

 
 
 
 

Branding Colour

We used blue with a purple hue, which enhanced its vibrancy. The primary reason we adopted blue as the primary colour was because it is considered to be the safest colour globally.

In addition, it is also a universal colour as it is deemed to be a useful colour. This is because a lot of colours from designer’s toolbox such as red, orange and green already have a strong built-in association - for error, caution/safety, success.

Therefore, this makes the blue colour a good choice for designers. Finally, blue colour aids in visually-impaired user. Prevalent types of colour blindness (Protanopia and Deuteranopia) can see the colour blue.

 
 
 
 

App Branding

The logo colour uses a mixture of blue and purple, forming a linear gradient - a band of colours that progress in a straight line. The use of gradient provides more creative freedom thus making bold statements.

Flat colours stifle design’s potential, gradients open the door to infinite possibilities thus aligning to our app’s objectives - to unleash homeowner’s creativity. The colour blending produces eye-catching and memorable visual designs by adding complexity and dimension to design, which helps in the app’s recognition.
